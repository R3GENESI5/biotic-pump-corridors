# 4. Discussion

## 4.1 Satellite signatures of the biotic pump

The biotic pump hypothesis (Makarieva and Gorshkov, 2007; Makarieva et al., 2013) predicts that forest evapotranspiration drives condensation that creates low pressure inland, drawing moist oceanic air along forest corridors. If this mechanism weakens, four observable consequences follow: (1) evapotranspiration declines, (2) cloud cover thins, (3) outgoing longwave radiation increases, and (4) column water vapour accumulates at the coast rather than penetrating inland.

Our results show all four signatures along the Belém–Manaus corridor. Critically, the signals are not uniformly distributed: they amplify with distance from the coast, the interior experiencing larger OLR increases (+4.3 W/m²), larger CRE decreases (−2.4 W/m²), and larger water vapour losses (−0.4 kg/m²) than the coastal segment. This spatial pattern — signal amplification with increasing distance from the oceanic moisture source — is unique to a transport failure mechanism. In contrast, a spatially uniform forcing such as increased CO₂ or a change in sea surface temperatures would produce more uniform changes along the corridor.

The AIRS water vapour transect provides particularly direct evidence. The coast gained moisture (+0.8 kg/m²) while the interior lost moisture (−0.4 kg/m²), steepening the coast-to-interior gradient by −1.2 kg/m². Moisture that should have been relayed inland instead accumulated near the coast. This is precisely what occurs when the condensation-driven pressure gradient that normally pulls air inland weakens: oceanic evaporation continues, but the atmospheric circulation that transports this moisture is diminished.

## 4.2 The SON transition: where the biotic pump operates

The seasonal decomposition strengthens the case for biotic pump involvement. The corridor signal is not distributed evenly across the year but concentrated in SON — the dry-to-wet transition season. During this period, OLR increased by +24.5 W/m² at the interior of the Amazon corridor, cloud fraction dropped 9%, and water vapour fell 2.4 kg/m².

This seasonality is significant in the context of the biotic pump framework. Multiple studies have documented that the Amazon wet season begins before the southward migration of the ITCZ reaches the region (Li and Fu, 2004; Wright et al., 2017). The early wet season onset has been attributed to increasing evapotranspiration and boundary-layer moistening that triggers deep convection, effectively pulling the monsoon onset inland through vegetation-atmosphere coupling. Our data show that in 2023, this mechanism failed: the SON transition did not produce the expected convective intensification at the interior end of the corridor.

During DJF, when the ITCZ provides geophysical forcing for convection, the corridor partially recovered — interior OLR declined and water vapour increased. This seasonal contrast — biotic pump failure in SON, geophysical compensation in DJF — is difficult to explain by any mechanism other than reduced vegetation-driven moisture convergence.

## 4.3 A progressive trend, not a single event

The 25-year CERES record reveals that the corridor weakening in 2023 is not an isolated event but part of a progressive multi-decadal degradation. The Amazon cloud fraction gradient has declined at −0.17%/yr over 2001–2025, and this trend is statistically significant after controlling for ENSO (p = 0.001). The CRE gradient trend (p = 0.033) is also significant.

The year-by-year data show a ratchet pattern: drought events produce sharp gradient reductions from which the corridor only partially recovers. Between 2005 and 2023, interior cloud fraction fell from 82.7% to 77.2% while the coast remained relatively stable. Each successive drought — 2005, 2010, 2015, 2023 — left the interior weaker than before.

This pattern is consistent with a positive feedback loop: drought reduces transpiration, which weakens the condensation-driven pressure gradient, which reduces inland moisture transport, which exacerbates drought. Such feedbacks have been proposed in theoretical studies of forest-climate tipping points (Lenton et al., 2008; Staal et al., 2020; Zemp et al., 2017) but have not previously been documented in satellite observations of the specific atmospheric variables predicted by the biotic pump framework.

## 4.4 Amazon and Congo: different stages of the same process

The Amazon and Congo corridors show degradation in the same direction but at different magnitudes. The Amazon exhibits strong signal amplification toward the interior, significant long-term trends, and declining ET and NDVI in intact forest. The Congo shows uniform modest increases in OLR, stable or slightly increasing ET, and increased NDVI in forest cells.

We interpret this as the two basins occupying different positions on the same degradation trajectory. The Amazon has experienced substantially greater deforestation and more frequent severe droughts over the past two decades (Marengo et al., 2018), placing it further along the feedback loop. The Congo, while experiencing growing pressure from land-use change and climate variability, retains more intact forest cover and its biotic pump appears to be weakened but still functional.

This interpretation carries an implication: the Congo Basin may be approaching a degradation threshold that the Amazon has already crossed. The declining CRE gradient in the Congo (−0.07 W/m²/yr, though not individually significant) suggests that the atmospheric response is beginning to decouple from the still-active forest, as occurred earlier in the Amazon. Continued monitoring of the Congo corridor is warranted.

## 4.5 Moisture displacement and extreme weather

During September 2023, while OLR showed large positive anomalies over the Amazon (+12.5 W/m² relative to the 2001–2018 climatology) and Congo (+5.0 W/m²), the eastern Mediterranean showed negative OLR anomalies (−4.6 W/m²) coinciding with Storm Daniel, which caused catastrophic flooding in Libya. A similar spatial dipole appeared in September 2024, with tropical positive anomalies and negative anomalies over Central Europe coinciding with Storm Boris.

We note this spatial coincidence as a hypothesis for future investigation rather than a claim of causation. The atmospheric dynamics linking tropical moisture sources to extratropical extreme precipitation involve complex teleconnections, jet stream interactions, and moisture transport pathways that cannot be resolved from the monthly satellite data used here. Moisture source attribution through back-trajectory analysis (e.g., HYSPLIT; Stein et al., 2015) and comparison with reanalysis-based moisture transport fields would be needed to test whether moisture displaced from weakened tropical forests contributed to these events.

## 4.6 Limitations

Several limitations of this study should be noted. First, the comparison of 2019 and 2023 captures the drought signal but does not characterise the full range of interannual variability. The 25-year trend analysis partially addresses this, but only for CERES-derived variables. Extending the MODIS ET and AIRS analyses to the full record would strengthen the temporal dimension.

Second, CERES data at 1-degree resolution cannot resolve sub-grid processes such as mesoscale convective organisation or land-breeze circulations that may modulate the biotic pump locally. The pixel-level correlation between ET and cloud fraction is weak (r = 0.04), suggesting that the pump operates at scales larger than individual grid cells. The corridor transect approach, which integrates across a latitude band, is better suited to the spatial scale of the mechanism.

Third, the MODIS ET product (MOD16A3GF) has known biases in tropical forests, including sensitivity to cloud contamination of input radiation data and reliance on meteorological reanalysis for some driving variables (Mu et al., 2011). The ET values should be interpreted as relative changes rather than absolute fluxes.

Fourth, while we control for ENSO using the ONI, other modes of climate variability (the Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation, the Indian Ocean Dipole) could contribute to the observed trends. The strong ENSO independence of the cloud fraction trend (ONI explains <1% of variance) suggests that large-scale ocean variability is not a primary driver, but this does not exclude all possible confounders.

Finally, satellite observations alone cannot establish causality between forest transpiration and atmospheric circulation changes. The correlations and spatial patterns we document are consistent with the biotic pump mechanism, but confirming the direction of causation requires Earth system model experiments with and without the condensation-driven pressure gradient.
