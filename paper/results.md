# 3. Results

## 3.1 Regional overview: 2019 versus 2023

Global mean OLR increased by 0.16 W/m² between 2019 and 2023. Against this background, the Eastern Amazon showed an OLR increase of +2.9 W/m² — 18 times the global mean — while the Congo Basin increased by +1.3 W/m² (Table 1). Cloud area fraction declined by 2.3% over the Eastern Amazon and 0.5% over the Congo. The longwave cloud radiative effect decreased by 1.5 W/m² (Amazon) and 0.7 W/m² (Congo), indicating that clouds trapped less longwave radiation in 2023 than in 2019.

MODIS evapotranspiration declined by 16 mm/yr (−1.1%) across the wider Eastern Amazon and by 66 mm/yr (−4.5%) in the Marabá subregion. In the Congo Basin, ET increased slightly (+13 mm/yr, +1.0%). The Eastern Amazon OLR difference map reveals a spatially coherent pattern of increased OLR across nearly the entire domain, with the strongest signals in the arc from Marabá southwestward toward the interior (Figure 2).

## 3.2 Corridor transect analysis

Along the Belém–Manaus corridor, OLR increased at every longitude, but the increase was substantially larger in the interior (+4.3 W/m² near Manaus) than at the coast (+2.7 W/m² near Belém) (Figure 3b). The coast-to-interior OLR gradient weakened by +1.6 W/m², meaning the interior lost part of its convective advantage over the coast.

Cloud fraction decreased by 3.6% in the mid-corridor (Marabá–Altamira) — the largest decline along the corridor — while the interior showed a smaller decrease of 1.1%. The longwave cloud radiative effect declined uniformly along the corridor, with the largest decreases at the interior (−2.4 W/m²).

AIRS column water vapour revealed a striking pattern: the coast gained moisture (+0.8 kg/m²) while the interior lost moisture (−0.4 kg/m²). The coast-to-interior water vapour gradient steepened by −1.2 kg/m², from −2.5 kg/m² in 2019 to −3.7 kg/m² in 2023. This indicates that oceanic moisture accumulated at the coast but failed to penetrate inland — the signature of weakened inland moisture transport (Figure 3b).

Along the Congo corridor (Gulf of Guinea to Kisangani), OLR increased by +1.3 to +1.6 W/m² with a more uniform distribution along the transect (Figure 3c). Cloud fraction and CRE declined modestly. Water vapour changes were small and did not show the strong coast-interior asymmetry observed in the Amazon. The Congo corridor appears to have experienced a weaker, more uniform perturbation compared to the sharp inland amplification in the Amazon.

## 3.3 Seasonal decomposition

The seasonal decomposition of OLR changes along the Amazon corridor reveals that the dry-to-wet transition season (SON: September–November) dominates the annual signal (Figure 4). Interior OLR increased by +24.5 W/m² during SON — an order of magnitude larger than the annual mean change — while mid-corridor OLR increased by +14.5 W/m² and coastal OLR by +10.5 W/m².

During the wet season (DJF), the pattern reversed: interior OLR decreased by −8.5 W/m², indicating enhanced convection associated with the arrival of the ITCZ. Cloud fraction during SON declined by 9.0% at the interior, compared to 6.1% at the coast.

AIRS water vapour showed the same seasonal concentration: during SON, the interior lost 2.4 kg/m² of column water vapour, while the coast showed negligible change. During DJF (wet season), all segments gained water vapour, with the interior recovering by +1.9 kg/m².

The concentration of the signal in SON is significant because this is the period when, according to the biotic pump framework, forest evapotranspiration should be initiating inland moisture convergence ahead of the ITCZ. The Amazon wet season onset typically precedes the seasonal arrival of the ITCZ by several weeks, driven by increasing transpiration and boundary-layer moistening (Wright et al., 2017; Fu et al., 2013). The 2023 data show this initiation mechanism failing at the interior end of the corridor.

The Congo showed a similar but weaker seasonal pattern, with JJA (boreal summer dry season) showing the largest OLR increase rather than SON.

## 3.4 Multi-year trends (2001–2025)

Over the 25-year CERES record, the Amazon corridor shows progressive weakening of the coast-to-interior gradient (Figure 5). The cloud fraction gradient declined at −0.17%/yr, and the CRE gradient declined at −0.12 W/m²/yr. The OLR gradient increased at +0.07 W/m²/yr, indicating that the interior is warming relative to the coast.

The year-by-year data show a ratchet pattern: drought years (2005, 2010, 2015, 2023) produce sharp gradient reductions, and recovery between events is incomplete. The interior cloud fraction was 82.7% in 2005 and had fallen to 77.2% by 2023, while the coast remained relatively stable (69–71%).

The Congo corridor shows trends in the same direction but with smaller magnitudes: OLR gradient +0.01 W/m²/yr, cloud fraction gradient −0.004%/yr, CRE gradient −0.07 W/m²/yr. None reach statistical significance individually, consistent with the Congo being at an earlier stage of degradation.

## 3.5 ENSO independence

The correlation between annual mean ONI and the Amazon cloud fraction gradient is r = 0.015 (p = 0.94) — effectively zero (Figure 5). ENSO explains less than 1% of the variance in the coast-to-interior cloud gradient. The correlation between ONI and the OLR gradient is similarly weak (r = −0.07, p = 0.74).

After regressing out the ONI signal, the ENSO-adjusted trends are virtually unchanged from the raw trends:

| Metric | Raw trend | ENSO-adjusted trend | p-value |
|--------|-----------|-------------------|---------|
| Cloud fraction gradient | −0.170%/yr | −0.170%/yr | 0.001 |
| CRE gradient | −0.116 W/m²/yr | −0.117 W/m²/yr | 0.033 |
| OLR gradient | +0.074 W/m²/yr | +0.075 W/m²/yr | 0.201 |

The cloud fraction gradient trend is significant at p = 0.001 and the CRE gradient at p = 0.033 after removing the ENSO signal. Comparison of 2023 with other El Niño years (2003, 2005, 2010, 2015–16, 2019) shows that the 2023 corridor metrics are close to the El Niño ensemble mean, rather than being anomalous outliers. The corridor degradation in 2023 reflects the continuation of a long-term trend, not an exceptional El Niño response.

## 3.6 Forest integrity: drought stress, not deforestation

Among the 225 grid cells classified as dense forest (NDVI > 0.6 in 2019) in the wider Eastern Amazon domain, 88.4% showed NDVI decline between 2019 and 2023 (Figure 6). The mean NDVI change in forest cells was −0.009 (t = −15.4, p < 10⁻⁶). The small number of non-forest cells (n = 6, NDVI 0.2–0.6) showed a slight NDVI increase (+0.004).

The Congo Basin showed the opposite pattern: NDVI in forest cells increased by +0.008 (t = +9.4, p < 10⁻⁶), with 78.5% of cells showing greening. This is consistent with the Congo maintaining its biotic pump function (ET stable or increasing) while the atmospheric response begins to weaken.

NDVI change and ET change are positively correlated across the Eastern Amazon (r = 0.29, p = 0.0006, n = 135), confirming that vegetation greenness decline predicts evapotranspiration decline at the grid-cell level. This establishes that the ET reduction documented in Section 3.1 reflects drought-driven suppression of transpiration in standing forest, not the removal of forest cover.

## 3.7 Spatial correlation between ET and OLR

Across 159 grid cells in the wider Eastern Amazon, the change in ET and the change in OLR are positively correlated (r = 0.32, p < 0.0001; Figure 7). Grid cells where ET declined more strongly also experienced larger OLR increases. This is consistent with the mechanistic chain: reduced transpiration → less boundary-layer moistening → reduced condensation and cloud formation → increased outgoing longwave radiation.

Forty-seven percent of cells fall in the "pump failure" quadrant (ET < 0, OLR > 0). The explained variance (r² = 0.10) is modest, reflecting the fact that OLR responds to the entire atmospheric column and moisture transported from upwind cells, not solely to local ET. The biotic pump operates as a circulation-scale mechanism, and the pixel-level correlation captures only the local component of a larger spatial process. The corridor-scale analyses presented in Sections 3.2–3.4 provide a more appropriate spatial framework for testing the mechanism.
